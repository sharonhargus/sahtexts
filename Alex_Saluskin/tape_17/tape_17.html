These texts consist of five Coyote legends and one legend about Cottontail and Grizzly. 
Alex Saluskin grew up in the Wenatchee area speaking a slightly different dialect, Pshwánwapam. He
moved to the lower Yakima River area at a young age, and became bidialectal. However, Virginia said
(12-2-23) of his speech: "It's a different dialect. That's the way his aunts spoke, and his
grandmother. Sometimes I couldn't understand certain words." Most of the time in these texts his
words are the same as the Yakmuɬáma (the Yakama people), but sometimes he pronounces them slightly
or entirely differently. He uses more non-Yakama forms in the Coyote legends than in the Cottontail
and Grizzly legend. He mentions that he learned the Coyote legends from certain Pshwánwapam
relatives but doesn't say who learned the Cottontail and Grizzly legend from. When Alex uses a
non-Yakama form, the Yakama equivalent is also provided whenever known, and an index of all such
words is provided at the end. 
Also, sometimes Alex deliberately uses Columbia River (Umatilla dialect) forms when talking about
Coyote or in direct quote from Coyote or his sisters, perhaps implying that they speak a different
dialect. These too are flagged.
This recording was made by Bruce Rigsby. He and Alex apparently never had time to transcribe and
translate it, but when he learned that Virginia and Sharon were going through texts, he alerted us
to the two texts by Alex Saluskin in the UW Libraries archive and encouraged us to work through
them. Rigsby responds periodically to Alex with "íi" (yes), not transcribed below. Recorded time of
texts: 1:03:30. These texts were initially translated by Virginia Beavert, and subsequently
transcribed and glossed by Sharon Hargus, with Virginia's help in places. After the transcription
and word/phrase glossing process was finished, sometimes the translation of the whole sentence was
adjusted (by Sharon).
